MRR vs ARR: Which SaaS Metric Matters More for Your Business?

Last updated: March 4, 2026 | 10 min read

If you're building a SaaS business, you've probably heard the debate: MRR or ARR? Monthly Recurring Revenue vs Annual Recurring Revenue. Which one should you focus on?

Here's the short answer: it depends on your business model, stage, and who you're talking to. But understanding when to use each—and why they both matter—can make the difference between clear communication and confusion.

Quick Takeaway

MRR is for operational management. ARR is for investor communication and enterprise sales. Both track the same underlying revenue, but they serve different audiences and purposes.

What is MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)?

Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR) is the total revenue you expect to receive each month from your subscription customers. It's the pulse of your SaaS business—the number you check every morning.

MRR Formula
MRR = Sum of (Customers x Monthly Subscription Price)

MRR Components

  • New MRR: Revenue from brand new customers
  • Expansion MRR: Additional revenue from existing customers (upgrades, add-ons)
  • Contraction MRR: Lost revenue from downgrades
  • Churned MRR: Lost revenue from cancellations

Net New MRR gives you the complete picture:

Net New MRR
Net New MRR = New MRR + Expansion MRR - Contraction MRR - Churned MRR

Example MRR Calculation

A company with:

  • 100 customers on $50/month plan = $5,000 MRR
  • 20 customers on $200/month plan = $4,000 MRR
  • 5 enterprise customers on $2,000/month plan = $10,000 MRR

Total MRR = $19,000/month

What is ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)?

Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR) is the annualized version of your recurring revenue. It's what your MRR would add up to over a year, assuming no changes.

ARR Formula
ARR = MRR x 12

Simple, right? But ARR also includes revenue from annual contracts, regardless of payment terms:

  • A customer paying $12,000/year upfront = $12,000 ARR
  • A customer paying $1,000/month = $12,000 ARR
  • A customer paying $3,000/quarter = $12,000 ARR

ARR normalizes all recurring revenue to an annual basis.

Example ARR Calculation

Using the same company:

From MRR to ARR
ARR = $19,000 MRR x 12 = $228,000

When to Use MRR

1. Day-to-Day Operations

MRR is your operational heartbeat. Use it for:

  • Daily/weekly revenue tracking
  • Sales team targets and quotas
  • Marketing campaign ROI measurement
  • Churn analysis and response
  • Cash flow planning

2. Monthly-Billed Businesses

If your customers pay monthly, MRR is your natural metric:

  • Consumer SaaS (Netflix-style)
  • SMB-focused B2B tools
  • Product-led growth companies
  • Freemium-to-paid conversion tracking

3. Quick Trend Detection

MRR changes show up faster. A 5% drop in MRR is immediately visible, while ARR smooths it out over the year.

  • Seasonal patterns appear clearly
  • Churn spikes are visible immediately
  • Campaign impact is measurable quickly

When to Use ARR

1. Investor Communication

Investors think in ARR. It's the language of SaaS valuation:

  • Pitch decks and fundraising
  • Board meetings and updates
  • Valuation multiples (ARR x multiple)
  • Comparables and benchmarking

Investor Math

A company with $1M ARR valued at 10x revenue = $10M valuation. Investors rarely say "83K MRR at 120x monthly revenue"—they think in annual terms.

2. Enterprise Sales

Enterprise deals are typically annual contracts:

  • Annual contracts are quoted in ARR
  • Multi-year deals are ARR x years
  • Enterprise sales quotas are ARR-based
  • Renewals are tracked as ARR retained

3. Annual-Billed Businesses

If customers pay annually, ARR is your natural metric:

  • Enterprise B2B SaaS
  • Annual subscription businesses
  • Companies with long sales cycles
  • High-touch sales models

The MRR-to-ARR Conversion: Watch for Traps

Converting between MRR and ARR seems simple (multiply or divide by 12), but there are pitfalls:

Trap 1: Annual Prepaid Revenue

A customer pays $24,000 for a 2-year contract upfront. Is that:

  • $24,000 ARR (correct)
  • $48,000 ARR (wrong—double counting)

ARR measures annualized revenue, not total contract value.

Trap 2: Seasonal Businesses

If December MRR is $100K but July MRR is $70K, your "real" ARR isn't $1.2M. Consider:

  • Use trailing 12-month average
  • Report seasonality to investors
  • Track MRR trends separately

Trap 3: Non-Recurring Revenue

Don't include in MRR/ARR:

  • Setup fees and onboarding charges
  • Professional services
  • One-time purchases
  • Usage overages (unless committed)

Common Mistake

Some companies inflate ARR by including "contract value" instead of "recurring revenue." A $100K one-time implementation fee is not ARR—it's professional services revenue.

The Rule of 3x

Early-stage SaaS companies often target 3x year-over-year ARR growth. $100K → $300K → $900K → $2.7M is a classic "triple-triple-double-double" pattern.

MRR/ARR and Other SaaS Metrics

MRR and ARR are foundational metrics that feed into many others:

1. LTV (Lifetime Value)

LTV = ARPU x Customer Lifespan. ARPU is derived from MRR or ARR divided by customer count.

2. CAC Payback Period

How many months of MRR to recover customer acquisition cost?

CAC Payback (Months)
CAC / (MRR per customer x Gross Margin)

3. Net Revenue Retention (NRR)

Uses MRR or ARR to measure growth from existing customers. See our NRR Guide.

4. Rule of 40

ARR growth rate + Profit margin should exceed 40. See our Rule of 40 Guide.

How to Report MRR/ARR to Investors

Best Practices

  1. Lead with ARR in pitch decks—it's what investors expect
  2. Show MRR trends in operational updates—granularity builds trust
  3. Break out components: New, Expansion, Contraction, Churn
  4. Be consistent—use the same calculation method every time
  5. Explain anomalies—seasonality, big deals, pricing changes

Sample Investor Update Format

Monthly Investor Update
ARR: $1.2M (+$50K from last month)
MRR: $100K (+8% MoM growth)
New MRR: $15K | Expansion: $5K | Churn: $3K
Net New MRR: $17K

Common Questions About MRR vs ARR

Q: Should I use MRR or ARR for pricing?

A: If monthly billing, use MRR. If annual contracts, use ARR. Some companies show both: "$99/month ($1,188 billed annually)" to encourage annual commitments.

Q: What if I have both monthly and annual customers?

A: Calculate MRR for all (divide annual by 12), then multiply by 12 for ARR. Track separately to understand your mix.

Q: Do investors ever ask for MRR?

A: Sometimes, especially early-stage investors who want to see granular growth patterns. But ARR is the standard for valuation discussions.

Q: Is there such thing as "too much" MRR/ARR focus?

A: Yes. Obsessing over MRR can lead to unsustainable growth tactics (heavy discounts, poor-fit customers). Balance growth with unit economics and customer quality.

Key Takeaways

  1. MRR = Monthly focus, ARR = Annual focus—same underlying revenue, different time horizons
  2. Use MRR for operations—day-to-day tracking, sales targets, trend detection
  3. Use ARR for investors—fundraising, board meetings, valuation discussions
  4. Convert correctly—ARR = MRR x 12, but watch for prepaid and seasonal issues
  5. Report both—gives stakeholders the full picture
  6. Don't game the metric—exclude non-recurring revenue

The best SaaS founders know when to use each metric. They track MRR daily for operational decisions and present ARR when talking to investors. Both numbers tell the same story—just from different time perspectives.

Master both, and you'll speak the language of SaaS fluently.
